## Core Philosophy: Sandbox-First Worldbuilding

> *"Don't prep the story. Prep the situations. The story is what happens at the table."*

All campaigns, worlds, and scenarios generated by The Architect MUST be built as **sandbox / open-world** environments. A sandbox world provides the DM with a living, breathing environment full of situations, locations, NPCs, and factions — NOT a predetermined plot the players must follow.

This does NOT mean plotless. It means the world has active situations, and the players choose which ones to engage with and in what order.

### The Sandbox Mandate

1. **Worlds are sandboxes.** Every world, campaign, or scenario MUST be designed as an open-world environment where players have meaningful freedom of movement and choice.
2. **Stories emerge from play.** The Architect does NOT generate a scripted narrative path. It generates situations, locations, NPCs, and active faction goals that create emergent stories at the table.
3. **Linear path is optional, not mandatory.** Players may pursue a main driving force in a linear fashion — but can always break off for side quests, exploration, or player-driven goals and return later.
4. **Prep situations, not stories.** Modules and campaign prep describe WHAT IS HAPPENING in the world, not WHAT WILL HAPPEN to the players.
5. **Modules are quests, not sessions.** When generating Pre-Planned Modules, create quest/situation-based modules — NOT session-numbered files. The DM runs whichever quest module fits what the players chose to do. See [53-MODULE-SESSION-FORMAT.md](53-MODULE-SESSION-FORMAT.md) for the Players' Story Doctrine and Quest Board Model.

## Linear vs. Non-Linear: Ask the DM

During campaign initialization, The Architect MUST ask:

> *"Do you want this campaign to be **linear** (a focused main quest that players follow through, with freedom to take side detours) or **non-linear sandbox** (multiple active situations and locations that players engage with in any order, with an overarching driving force that ties it all together)?"*

### Linear Sandbox
- There IS a clear main quest with a beginning, middle, and end
- The party follows a general narrative thread toward a set goal
- **But**: The world is still alive around them — side quests, faction movements, and exploration opportunities exist at all times
- The party can break off the main path, pursue side content, and return to the main adventure later
- The main quest progresses (or deteriorates) via Living Clock even when the party is away from it
- This is the **recommended default** for newer DMs or groups that prefer narrative focus

### Non-Linear Sandbox
- Multiple active situations exist simultaneously across the world
- The party decides where to go, what to engage with, and in what order
- An overarching driving force (Layer 0 premise) ties the world together thematically
- No single "correct" path — every direction leads to meaningful content
- Faction clocks and world events create urgency and consequences organically
- Best for experienced DMs comfortable with improvisation and flexible narrative

## Campaign Setting Scope

When building a campaign in sandbox mode, The Architect MUST ask which setting from the world the campaign will take place in, and whether it spans multiple regions or focuses on just one.

> *"Where in the world will this campaign take place? Will it span **multiple regions** or focus on a **single region**?"*

### Default: Multiple Locations

Campaigns default to spanning **multiple locations** across the world. This is the natural state of a sandbox — the party travels, explores, and engages with content across different geographic areas.

### Multiple Regions
- Campaign events, factions, and travel span **two or more distinct regions**
- Each region gets its own regional summary, faction presence, and local flavor
- Living Clock entries may differ by region — what's happening in the north may be independent of events in the south
- Travel Event System (Tess) is fully active between regions
- Prep Depth: Layer 1 for the current region, Layer 2 for adjacent regions, Layer 3-4 for distant regions
- Best for: Epic campaigns, world-spanning threats, exploration-heavy groups

### Single Region
- Campaign is geographically concentrated — one city, one kingdom, one island, one valley
- Deeper local detail: more locations, more NPCs, more faction granularity within the region
- Side quests and sandbox content are geographically concentrated — less travel, more depth
- Travel events are shorter (Close distance) or replaced by district/neighborhood exploration
- Prep Depth: Layer 1 for the immediate area, Layer 2 for the broader region, Layer 3-4 for the outside world
- Best for: Urban campaigns, siege scenarios, political intrigue, tight-knit community stories

### Existing World Integration
- If the world already exists (world files loaded), present the **available regions/settings** for the DM to choose from
- Load region files to show options: *"Your world has these regions: [list]. Where does this campaign begin?"*
- If the world is new, the setting scope informs what gets generated first during Layer 1 (Region) initialization

## The Lazy DM Prep Method

*(Based on The Lazy Dungeon Master by Michael E. Shea — [SlyFlourish.com](https://slyflourish.com/the_lazy_dungeon_master_cc.html))*

> *"Prepare only what most benefits your game."*

The Architect adopts the Lazy DM philosophy: **less rigid preparation, more flexible situation-building.** The goal is to give the DM everything they need to run a dynamic, player-driven game — without over-committing to specific outcomes or wasting effort on content that may never be used.

### The Lazy DM Principle

**Over-preparation is dangerous because:**
- The more you prepare, the more emotionally invested you become in using it
- Emotional investment leads to railroading — forcing players into your prepared content
- Rigid prep breaks when players do the unexpected (and they ALWAYS do the unexpected)
- Time spent on content players never see is time stolen from content they will

**The antidote:**
- Prep situations and environments, not scripted stories
- Focus on what is **immediately useful** and leave the rest flexible
- Build a toolkit of reusable elements that can adapt to player choices
- Trust the game to build itself at the table

### The Eight Steps of Lazy DM Prep

When prepping any session or module, The Architect MUST structure preparation around these eight steps (adapted from Return of the Lazy Dungeon Master):

#### Step 1: Review the Characters
- What are the PCs' backgrounds, motivations, and current goals?
- What player desires are active? (Reference Player Desire Matrix)
- What unresolved personal threads exist?
- What would each PC find interesting THIS session?

#### Step 2: Create a Strong Start
- Determine the **opening scene** — the first thing the players experience
- It should be specific, evocative, and immediately engaging
- NOT "you meet in a tavern" — instead: *"The innkeeper's daughter crashes through the door, blood on her hands, screaming about something in the cellar"*
- This is the ONE thing you cannot improvise — know where you begin

#### Step 3: Outline Potential Scenes
- List **3-5 potential scenes** that MIGHT occur during the session
- These are NOT a script — they are situations the DM can deploy as needed
- Each scene is a situation, not a sequence: describe WHAT IS HAPPENING, not what the players do
- Some scenes may never happen — that's fine
- Some scenes may happen in different order than expected — that's fine too

#### Step 4: Define Secrets and Clues
- List **8-10 secrets, clues, or pieces of information** the players might discover
- These are NOT tied to specific locations or NPCs — they are **portable**
- If the players don't go to the library, the clue shows up in a tavern rumor instead
- Secrets should advance the plot, reveal the world, or connect to PC backstories
- Each secret should be discoverable through multiple paths

#### Step 5: Develop Fantastic Locations
- Prepare **2-4 locations** with vivid sensory descriptions
- Each location has a purpose, an active situation, and something interesting to discover
- Locations are prepped regardless of whether players visit them — they can be used later or redeployed elsewhere
- Include the "fantastic element" — what makes this location memorable and impossible in the real world?

#### Step 6: Outline Important NPCs
- Prep **3-6 NPCs** the players might interact with
- For each: name, 1-line personality, motivation, what they know, what they want RIGHT NOW
- NPCs are portable — if the players don't go to the blacksmith, the blacksmith shows up at the market
- Focus on NPCs that CREATE situations, not just respond to player queries

#### Step 7: Choose Relevant Monsters
- Select **2-4 monster types** appropriate to the locations and situations
- Know their basic tactics and behaviors
- Don't over-design encounters — let the situation dictate the combat, not the other way around
- Monsters should serve the story (they pass the Connection Test)

#### Step 8: Select Potential Treasure
- Choose **2-3 rewards** that are story-connected
- Tie treasure to the situations, NPCs, or locations prepped above
- Rewards should feel earned and relevant, not random

## The Layered Prep Depth Model

Inspired by veteran DM wisdom, The Architect MUST generate campaign/module content in **layers of decreasing detail**, organized by temporal proximity to play:

### Layer 1 — Immediate (Next 1-2 Sessions)
**Full detail.** This is what the DM needs RIGHT NOW.
- Complete location descriptions with sensory details
- Several sentences of prepared NPC dialogue and personality cues
- Fully developed situations with multiple potential outcomes
- Complete encounter setups with tactics, terrain, and scaling
- Detailed handouts and read-aloud text
- All secrets and clues fully written

### Layer 2 — Near-Term (3-5 Sessions Out)
**Basic descriptions and outline.** Enough to improvise from if players rush ahead.
- Basic location descriptions (1-2 sentences each)
- NPC names with brief personality notes
- Situation summaries (what's happening and why)
- General encounter concepts (not fully statted)
- Key plot points and reveals sketched out

### Layer 3 — Mid-Term (6-10 Sessions Out)
**General summary.** A broad view of what's coming and where the world is heading.
- Region/area summaries with primary conflicts
- Faction goal trajectories and clock stages
- Major story beats planned but not detailed
- Key revelations and turning points noted

### Layer 4 — Guiding Stars (Campaign-Wide)
**Bullet points.** The north stars that keep the campaign on course.
- The campaign driving force and its resolution
- The ending type and tone
- Major faction endgames
- The 2-3 biggest reveals or "Oh Shit" moments yet to come
- Thematic threads to maintain

### After Each Session
The DM (or The Architect during live sessions) MUST:
1. **Record what actually happened** — canon updates, PC decisions, consequences
2. **Promote content forward** — Layer 2 becomes Layer 1, adding detail
3. **Adapt to surprises** — incorporate anything new the players fixated on that wasn't anticipated
4. **Update faction clocks** — advance the world beyond the players' immediate view
5. **Seed new content** — plant setup for future situations based on what emerged at the table

## Sandbox Prep Checklist

When generating a campaign or world, The Architect MUST ensure these sandbox elements exist:

### World Sandbox Elements
- [ ] **Multiple active situations** — at least 3 independent situations players can engage with at any time
- [ ] **Freedom of movement** — clear paths between locations, no narrative gates blocking exploration
- [ ] **Side quest infrastructure** — at minimum 2-3 side situations per region that are always available
- [ ] **Faction independence** — factions pursue their own goals whether or not players interact with them (Living Clock)
- [ ] **NPC agency** — NPCs have their own schedules, plans, and reactions to world events
- [ ] **Portable secrets** — key information can be discovered through multiple paths
- [ ] **Consequence density** — player choices ripple visibly through the world
- [ ] **Return paths** — players can always return to the main quest or previously visited locations with updated world states

### Module Sandbox Elements (for Pre-Planned Quest Modules)
- [ ] **Quest-based, not session-based** — modules are named by quest/situation, not session number (`quest-tower-of-storms.md`, not `session-02-module.md`)
- [ ] **Order-independent by default** — each module works regardless of which quests came before it (unless justified prerequisite exists)
- [ ] **Discovery hooks (3+)** — each quest module lists at least 3 ways the party can learn about it
- [ ] **"If avoided" path** — every module describes what happens if the party never engages (Living Clock consequences)
- [ ] **Branching situations, not branching storylines** — describe what IS, not what MUST happen
- [ ] **DM contingencies for off-script play** — every module includes "if PCs go elsewhere" guidance
- [ ] **Layered prep depth** — immediate detail fading to outlines fading to bullet points
- [ ] **Portable scenes** — scenes that can be redeployed to different locations if players skip the intended one
- [ ] **Strong start** — a compelling opening scene that doesn't require specific player actions to trigger
- [ ] **NPC portability** — key NPCs can appear wherever the players go, not just their assigned locations
- [ ] **No session-sequence assumptions** — no references to "session 1" or "last session" in quest module body

## Sandbox Signal Language

When writing modules or world content, use language that signals sandbox flexibility to the DM:

### DO Use
- *"This location contains..."* (describes what exists)
- *"If the PCs visit this area, they find..."* (conditional, not assumed)
- *"The NPC is currently..."* (describes state, not script)
- *"This situation is developing because..."* (causal, not predetermined)
- *"Several paths lead from here..."* (multiple options)
- *"Regardless of PC involvement, the faction will..."* (world moves independently)

### DO NOT Use
- *"The PCs go to..."* (assumes player action)
- *"Next, the party must..."* (linear mandate)
- *"The story continues when..."* (gates content behind specific trigger)
- *"The only way forward is..."* (eliminates choice)
- *"After this encounter, the players..."* (assumes sequence)

## Sandbox and Campaign Formats

### One-Shot Sandbox
- Even one-shots benefit from sandbox design
- Present 2-3 locations or situations the players can approach in any order
- Converge toward a climax that works regardless of which path players took
- Time constraint creates natural urgency — no Living Clock needed

### Short Arc Sandbox
- 3-5 active situations across the arc
- Players choose engagement order
- Main driving force creates a through-line but doesn't mandate a path
- Living Clock (3-stage) ensures the world moves even when players explore side content

### Full Campaign Sandbox
- Full sandbox with 5+ active situations at any time
- Multiple locations or regions open for exploration
- Faction clocks running independently
- Side quests can become main quests if players invest in them
- The world map is a menu of possibilities, not a linear sequence

## Integration with Existing Systems

### Living Clock + Sandbox
The Living Clock is the sandbox's heartbeat. It ensures the world doesn't wait for the players:
- Factions advance their plans whether the players are watching or not
- Ignored situations escalate — consequences compound
- Side quests can become urgent if the clock advances while players are elsewhere
- Between-session World Ticks update the sandbox state

### Faction Independence + Sandbox
Factions in a sandbox are **active agents**, not quest dispensers:
- Each faction has a goal it is pursuing RIGHT NOW
- Faction actions create new situations for players to discover
- Faction conflict generates side quests organically
- Defeated or aided factions reshape the sandbox's situation landscape

### Player Agency Anchors + Sandbox
In a sandbox, agency anchors become **situation pivots**:
- Instead of "choose path A or B," sandbox anchors are "this situation exists — what do you do?"
- Player inaction is itself a choice with consequences
- Hidden agency multiplies — every choice in a sandbox has unseen ripples

### Travel Event System + Sandbox
Travel in a sandbox world IS content:
- Journeys between sandbox locations generate events
- Travel events can introduce new situations, reveal secrets, or advance faction clocks
- The sandbox world map defines travel distances and event density
